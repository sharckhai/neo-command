1. Executive Summary
The Core Insight
During observership at Lurie's Children's Hospital (Level III NICU), we observed that
coordination, not clinical knowledge, is the bottleneck in maternal-neonatal emergency
transport. A constellation of 12+ specialists surrounds every complex case, each contributing
domain expertise that must be synthesized into actionable decisions.
This multidisciplinary coordination exists at Level III academic medical centers but is absent in
rural/Level II facilities where most babies are born. The knowledge gap contributes directly to
maternal mortality disparities.

The Solution

NeoCommand is a multi-agent AI system that:
1.â€‹ Learns from Level III multidisciplinary coordination patterns
2.â€‹ Simulates specialist consultation in real-time (12+ agents debating like real specialists)
3.â€‹ Synthesizes complex input into clear recommendations
4.â€‹ Transfers knowledge from academic centers to rural providers who need it most

The Innovation: Simulation Observatory
Before deploying to real clinical scenarios, we're building a Simulation Observatory where:
â—â€‹
â—â€‹
â—â€‹
â—â€‹

Real specialists observe AI agents debating anonymized cases
Specialists validate whether AI reasoning matches clinical reality
Specialists maintain authority - they approve/override every decision
The system learns from specialist feedback in a controlled research environment

This is not automation. This is knowledge democratization with human oversight.

def synthesize_recommendation(self, all_specialist_input):

Conflict Resolution Heuristics
When specialists disagree, Head Coordinator applies:
1. Clinical Safety First
IF any specialist raises critical safety concern
THEN that concern overrides efficiency/speed considerations
UNLESS another specialist provides evidence that safety concern is mitigated

2. Specialty Deference
WHEN conflict is within a specialist's primary domain
THEN weight that specialist's input more heavily

EXAMPLE: MFM opinion on maternal hemorrhage > generalist OB opinion

3. Consensus Building
WHEN >70% of relevant specialists agree
THEN high confidence in that direction
WHEN 50-70% agreement
THEN moderate confidence, surface trade-offs clearly
WHEN <50% agreement
THEN flag as requiring human judgment, present multiple viable options

4. Time-Critical Tiebreaking
WHEN time is critical (maternal/neonatal deterioration)
AND conflict exists between "optimal" and "fast enough"
THEN favor "fast enough with acceptable safety margins"
BECAUSE perfect can be enemy of good in emergency medicine

5. Family-Centered Care Preference
WHEN clinical outcomes are similar between options
AND one option better preserves mother-child dyad
THEN favor keeping dyad together
UNLESS safety margins are unacceptable
